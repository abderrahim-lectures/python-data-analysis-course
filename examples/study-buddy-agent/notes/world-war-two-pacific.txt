World War II: The Pacific Theater, a Quick Overview

Japan's expansion into the Pacific accelerated through the 1930s, including
the invasion of Manchuria in 1931 and full-scale war with China from 1937.
Tensions with the United States rose as Japan pushed further into
Southeast Asia, seeking oil, rubber, and other resources it lacked at home.

On December 7, 1941, Japan launched a surprise attack on the American naval
base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, destroying or damaging much of the Pacific
Fleet's battleships. The United States declared war on Japan the next day,
formally entering World War II; Germany and Italy declared war on the US
days later, pulling the country into the European theater as well.

The Battle of Midway, in June 1942, is widely considered the turning point
in the Pacific. American codebreakers had partially cracked Japanese naval
codes, letting the US Navy anticipate the attack and ambush the Japanese
fleet. Japan lost four aircraft carriers in the battle, a loss it could
never fully replace, while the US lost one -- after Midway, Japan shifted
from offense to defense for the remainder of the war.

The US then pursued an "island-hopping" strategy: rather than retaking
every Japanese-held island, American forces selectively captured strategic
islands that could serve as air and naval bases for the next advance,
bypassing heavily fortified but less critical positions entirely. Key
battles in this campaign included Guadalcanal (1942-43), Tarawa (1943),
Saipan (1944), and Iwo Jima (early 1945) -- each fought at extremely high
cost on both sides.

The war in the Pacific ended after the United States dropped atomic bombs
on Hiroshima (August 6, 1945) and Nagasaki (August 9, 1945), the only use
of nuclear weapons in armed conflict to date. Japan announced its surrender
on August 15, 1945 (V-J Day), and formally signed the surrender document
aboard the USS Missouri on September 2, 1945, ending World War II.
